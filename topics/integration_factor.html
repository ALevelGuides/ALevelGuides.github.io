Integrating Factors

Some differentials equations can be written in the form:

\[\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x)g(y)\]

These are called seperable differential equations and are easily solvable. You should have already learned seperable differential equations in A-Level Maths.

Some first order differential equations are not solvable by seperation of variables. Here we will investigate how to solve differential equations in the form:

\[A(x)\frac{dy}{dx} + B(x)y = C(x)\]

We will solve these by using integrating factors.

First, we need to turn the coefficient of \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) as \(1\), we can do this by dividing by \(A(x)\)

\[\frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{B(x)}{A(x)}y = \frac{C(x)}{A(x)}\]

Notice since \(A(x)\), \(B(x)\) and \(C(x)\) are arbitrary functions, we can replace the fractions by other arbitrary functions which we shall name \(P\) and \(Q\):

\[\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)\]

For reasons we will see later, we now multiply both sides by the function \(I(x)\), which will be our integrating factor.

\[I(x)\frac{dy}{dx} + I(x)P(x)y = I(x)Q(x) \quad (*)\]

Essentially, our goal is to make the left hand side look like the product rule, take a look at the following derivative to see what I mean:

\[\frac{d}{dx}(y\cdot I(x)) = I(x)\frac{dy}{dx} + y\frac{dI}{dx}\]

If we equate this to the left hand side of (*), we get:

\[ \begin{aligned} &I(x)\frac{dy}{dx} + y\frac{dI}{dx} = I(x)\frac{dy}{dx} + I(x)P(x)y \\ \\ &\implies y\frac{dI}{dx} = I(x)P(x)y \\ \\ &\implies \frac{dI}{dx} = I(x)P(x) \end{aligned} \]

This is a seperable differential equation in terms of \(I(x)\), which we can quickly solve as:

\[ \begin{aligned} &\frac{dI}{dx} = I(x)P(x) \\ \\ &\frac{dI}{I} = P(x)dx \\ \\ &\int \frac{dI}{I} = \int P(x)dx \\ \\ &\ln{I} = \int P(x)dx \\ \\ &I(x) = e^{\int P(x)dx} \end{aligned} \]

This allows us to find \(I(x)\) given a \(P(x)\), also while solving this integral you don't need to add a \(+C\), as it will be irrelevant. Using this \(I(x)\) in (*) will turn the left hand side into the product rule.

\[\frac{d}{dx}(y\cdot I(x)) = I(x)Q(x)\]

Now the solution is clear, simply integrate both sides and divide by \(I(x)\)

\[ \begin{aligned} &\frac{d}{dx}(y\cdot I(x)) = I(x)Q(x) \\ \\ &y \cdot I(x) = \int I(x)Q(x)dx \\ \\ &y = \frac{1}{I(x)} \int I(x)Q(x)dx \end{aligned} \]

Finally, we have reached the solution. We cannot omit the \(+C\) in this integral, in order to get a general solution.

Here's an example problem:

Find the solution to the differential equation

\[\cos{x}\frac{dy}{dx} + y = \cos^2{x} + \sin{x} \; \; \text{where} \; \; 0 \leq x < \frac{\pi}{2}\]

given that \(y = 1\) when \(x = \frac{\pi}{3}\)

Solution

First let's divide both sides by \(cos{x}\).

\[ \begin{aligned} &\frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{1}{\cos{x}}y = \frac{\cos^2{x}}{\cos{x}} + \frac{\sin{x}}{\cos{x}} \\ \\ &\frac{dy}{dx} + y\sec{x} = \cos{x} + \tan{x} \\ \\ \end{aligned} \]

Hence we can find the integrating factor by:

\[ \begin{aligned} &I(x) = e^{\int P(x)dx}\\ \\ &I(x) = e^{\ln{(\sec{x} + \tan{x})}} \\ \\ &I(x) = \sec{x} + \tan{x} \end{aligned} \]

Now we can go back to the original differential equation.

\[ \begin{aligned} &\frac{d}{dx}(y \cdot (\sec{x} + \tan{x})) = (\cos{x} + \tan{x})(\sec{x} + \tan{x}) \\ \\ &= \cos{x}\sec{x} + \cos{x}\tan{x} + \tan{x}\sec{x}+ \tan^2{x} \\ \\ &= 1 + \sin{x} + \tan{x}\sec{x} + \sec^2{x} - 1 \\ \\ &= \sin{x} + \tan{x}\sec{x} + \sec^2{x} \\ \\ &\implies y = \frac{1}{\sec{x} + \tan{x}}\int ( \sin{x} + \tan{x}\sec{x} + \sec^2{x})dx \\ \\ &= \frac{\sec{x} + \tan{x} - \cos{x} + C}{\sec{x} + \tan{x}} \end{aligned} \]

We have found our general solution, now we can put in our initial conditions to get the final solution.

\[ \begin{aligned} &x = \frac{\pi}{3}, \; y = 1 \\ \\ &\implies \sec{x} = 2, \; \cos{x} = \frac{1}{2}, \; \tan{x} = \sqrt{3} \\ \\ &\implies 1 = \frac{2+\sqrt{3} - \frac{1}{2} + C}{2 + \sqrt{3}} \\ \\ &\implies C = \frac{1}{2} \\ \\ &\implies y = \frac{\sec{x} + \tan{x} - \cos{x} + \frac{1}{2}}{\sec{x} + \tan{x}} \end{aligned} \]
